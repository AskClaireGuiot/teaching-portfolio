Summary

This cheat sheet gives a quick look at the financial challenges Canadian post-secondary education (PSE) is facing, with a spotlight on North Island College (NIC), a community college on Vancouver Island, BC. This page covers trends in government funding, reliance on international student enrollment, and policy changes. Please use this as a guide to get up to speed on what’s happening and to help support advocacy for sustainable funding and stable programs.

Key Trends (2000–2025)

  • Government funding dropped from ~55% (2000) 1 to ~30–35% (2020s) 2.
  • Domestic tuition increases capped at 0–2% annually 3.
  • => International student fees have become a vital source of revenue to help fill the gap.
  • In 2024, the federal government introduced caps on new study permits and tightened eligibility for Post-Graduation Work Permits (PGWP) and Open Work Permits (OWP), aiming to reduce pressure on housing and immigration levels 7.
  • => As a result, international enrollment across Canada has dropped significantly—by an average of about 45%11.

Challenges & Policy Tension

  • International enrollment drops due to federal visa restrictions7.
  • Housing pressures in major cities constrain student numbers8.
  • Institutions struggle to balance budgets without high international tuition revenue4.
  • Government prioritizes permanent skilled immigration over temporary student flows9.

Recommended Responses for PSE

  • Diversify revenue: continuing education, micro-credentials, industry partnerships.
  • Innovate program delivery: hybrid/online learning, co-op programs, short-term certificates.
  • Retention & student experience: advising, support services, pathway programs.
  • Advocacy: engage government for temporary relief and predictable policy measures.

Long-Term Solution

Stable government funding is critical to reduce dependency on international tuition, maintain staff, and ensure quality education1,2.
